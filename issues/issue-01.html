What to See When You See a Tree

A tree is a small landscape: a condensed biography of slope, wind, water, and time written in wood and lichen.

Essay

What to See When You See a Tree: The Architecture of Attention

Regard a tree on any ridge of the Western Ghats, and the illusion of the solitary object dissolves. We are conditioned to see a plant - something tall, green, rooted, recognisable: a noun in a world of nouns. But stand long enough with one specimen, perhaps a towering Vateria indica rising from the valley floor, and the category collapses. You are not looking at a plant. You are looking at the visible edge of a much larger structure: the basalt slope beneath, the shear of wind above, the monsoon that carved the hillside, and the networks of fungi, insects, and birds that move invisibly through the wood. A tree is not a unit. It is the cross-section of a system.

Stone and Root

In the Western Ghats, this insight is not theoretical. It is felt. This mountain range receives the full force of the Arabian Sea weather systems, and the topography amplifies every relationship. The underlying geology is the Deccan Traps, a staircase of ancient basalt flows weathered into terraces, fracture lines, and narrow shelves. The stone is chemically austere and physically unforgiving. Because the ground offers little easily, the root of every tree becomes a strategist.

It does not simply penetrate the earth. It negotiates with it. On wind-scoured plateaus, roots etch the basalt with weak acids, dissolving mineral grain by grain over decades. On steep lateritic slopes, they abandon depth and weave laterally instead, forming tensile mats that grip the soil against the violence of the monsoon. When you look at the base of a tree in the Ghats, you are seeing the compromise between the biological desire for height and gravity’s constant attempt to tear it down.

Wind and Form

Higher up the ridge, the dominant force changes. It is no longer gravity but wind. The westerlies that hit the Ghats during the monsoon are not breezes. They are fluid hammers, striking the canopy thousands of times every hour. Trees record this punishment in their architecture. On an exposed shoulder, a tree flags to one side. Branches on the windward face are suppressed, while those on the sheltered side stretch outward in a long, unbalanced arc.

This is thigmomorphogenesis, growth shaped by repeated touch and pressure. The tree feels the force and reinforces its tissues, adding lignin where the bending moment is highest. Even a novice naturalist can sketch the wind map of a valley by studying the silhouettes at dusk.

Move into a sheltered shola forest and the form reverses. Here the wind is quiet, and the same species can rise in a rounded crown, soft and symmetrical. Sholas often show crown shyness, a phenomenon where neighbouring trees keep narrow gaps between their canopies. The form is different because the forces are different. The tree is a fluid diagram of the atmosphere.

Water and Light

Drop down into the humid evergreen zone and the bark becomes a record of moisture. On these slopes, trunks are coated with bryophytes, liverworts, and small ferns. Higher on the branches, orchids anchor themselves and draw water from the mist. This epiphytic load changes the physics of the tree entirely. At the height of the monsoon, a single canopy giant in these cloud-soaked forests can hold enormous volumes of water in its moss and root masses, sometimes measured in thousands of litres. Rain does not simply fall through these forests. It gets caught.

The tree becomes a vertical reservoir. It slows the rain, releases moisture gradually, and feeds the streams long after the season has ended.

Look closely at the bark and you will see a palimpsest. Crustose lichens form white and grey patches that track air quality and humidity. Foliose lichens appear where conditions are even cleaner. If the bark is peeling, the tree may be shedding its surface to free itself of vines or fungal crusts. Every square centimetre is a weather diary.

The leaves add finer resolution. In deep shade, the laminas are broad and soft, angled to catch diffuse light. On the exposed ridge, they become thick, waxy, or reduced to tough ovals to resist drying winds. The drip tips of rainforest species such as Hopea and many figs are not decorative. They serve a precise function: they channel water quickly, so algae does not form a film that blocks the light. Leaf angles signal more secrets. A slight downward tilt before the rains can precede the first thunderstorm by weeks. Trees respond to humidity and barometric cues with a sensitivity that surprises anyone who has watched them long enough.

The Forest Network

Trees are never alone. They host ants, beetles, cicadas, birds, and bats. These creatures act as indicators of tree health and larger ecological processes. A rhinoceros beetle at the base signals a functioning deadwood cycle. A chorus of bulbuls high in the canopy suggests a fig in fruit. The pitch of cicadas shifts with temperature and time of day. A silent trunk is often a warning. The soil beneath may be compacted by grazing, or a fire may have swept through years ago and broken the chain of decomposers.

Beneath the soil lies the most intricate layer of all. The roots of many Western Ghats species are entwined with mycorrhizal fungi. These filaments link trees into networks that move carbon and nutrients. This system is sometimes called the wood wide web, although the term can mislead. It is not a hive mind. It is closer to a marketplace shaped by constraints. A large Vateria indica in its prime may supply carbon to shaded saplings, and a sickly tree may contribute nutrients it cannot use. The forest floor becomes an economy where energy and minerals shift constantly, unseen yet essential.

Memory and Pattern

Seen through this wider frame, a tree stops being an object and becomes a node. Complexity science calls this emergence. Patterns arise from interactions, not from single components. A tree is shaped by rain only because the rain meets the slope at a particular angle, the soil holds a particular depth, the wind carries a certain force, the fungi supply a specific mineral profile, and the neighbouring canopies compete for light. Alter one input and the form adjusts. Not by choice, but by necessity.

A tree is also memory made visible. Inside the trunk, the rings record rainfall patterns. On the bark, scars record old fires. In the crown, broken branches show where a storm once passed. Along a single slope, trees may share a common history, but each carries that history with different emphases. The valley floor remembers the flood, and the ridge remembers the wind. This is why two adjacent valleys can feel like separate countries. The species may be the same, but the flows of light, moisture, and wind differ just enough to create new outcomes.

The Diagnostic Gaze

Once you learn to read trees this way, you cannot stop. Every walk becomes an investigation. Why is one crown leaning downhill while the others are straight? Why has lichen vanished from this patch? Why are there no saplings under a mature canopy? The answers often reveal changes in hydrology, soil structure, or human disturbance. These questions are not academic. They are practical tools for reading terrain and predicting the movement of water and wind.

A Conversation with the Mountain

The Western Ghats demand this literacy. Steep, narrow, rain-fed, and full of microclimates, they reward close attention. A tree does not stand alone. It stands with the rock, the cloud, the wind, the fungi, and the fauna. It is a conversation between the soil and the sky, and the conversation never stops.

And so, what about that Vateria indica? If you take one habit from this issue, let it be this: pause before the tree. Do not just name it. Naming ends the inquiry. Instead, ask what shaped it, what it is responding to, and what it is recording. Trees do not speak in words. They speak in form. That form is never random. It is the visible expression of a relationship. To see the tree is to see the system - and to realise how much still remains unseen. From that moment on, you read the Ghats themselves differently.

Back to the Vateria indica

With these thoughts, I walk up to the Vateria indica I know, beside a housing complex called “Forest Life,” on the outskirts of Pune. This was once the last line of buildings before the scrub and slope began. The tree had stood just beyond the township wall, a single column of shade at the edge of the plateau, and I had filed it away as a future lesson in how basalt, wind, and monsoon write themselves into wood. The last time I had seen it was six months earlier, at the start of the rains, when cicadas were still loud in the gullies and the soil held the promise of fresh life.

This time there is no shade, no trunk, no lichen script to read. In its place is a levelled, compacted rectangle of earth, ringed with steel rods and heaps of brick, a bare stage waiting for concrete. The soil holds marks of truck-tyres. At the spot where the tree stood, a flex board leans in the dust:

WELCOME TO THE FOREST
Studio apartments and villas - best rates!

Whatever that Vateria indica had been recording - the slow negotiation of root and rock, the years of wind, the rise and fall of groundwater - has been erased in a season, its memory flattened into a plot number and a marketing line. The ridge will still remember the monsoon, but now through drains and parapets instead of bark and crown. That is what has become of the Vateria indica of Bhugaon. This, too, is part of what you see when you look closely at a tree.
